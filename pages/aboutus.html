A little bit about us

Consumerism has become the norm in the West. It is a world in which we live to shop, fuelled by an ever-increasing number of shopping malls and mega centres, and now the internet. It is further facilitated by the endless media advertising campaigns tempting us with all manner of goods, catering our every need. It is also a world of the disposable, from paper cups to plastic bags and bottles, to computers and cars, all created on the premise of speed, convenience and obsolescence. If and object no longer works or has ceased to be attractive, it can easily be replaced with a newer version. In a world of the new then, with such a vast array of consumer choice, why is consumption of all things second-hand so popular today? What is it about the material culture of the second hand that makes it so enduring? What are the reasons for this?

– Changing Patterns of Consumerism: The Rise and Rise of the Second Hand – Deborah Jan Noon, Massey University (Wellington, New Zealand)


  We are a community of people who believe our consumption habits could be key to build a more sustainable world. We want to contribute to the change our generation can make in the global economy, by reusing in addition to recycling and being conscious about the environmental impact our consuming habits. Giving a second opportunity to good quality products, including electronics, clothes, toys, books, furniture, and even bicycles and cars, will save you some money and help you to make of this world a better place.

  If all these is sounding a bit of too hippy for you, we want you to know that this is not all about peace and love. It is also about being practical. We want to put you in contact with other people that are near you that are looking for what you’re selling. We are thinking about that sweater your Auntie Doris got you last Christmas or all that stuff you even didn’t know it was there until you moved into your new apartment. Remember, moving three times is as bad as a fire. So, instead of burning down all those things or throw them away, maybe you can make a little profit, while somebody saves some money and you both contribute to a more sustainable economy. Everybody wins :-)

  Second-hand markets are not moth-eaten place anymore. We have changed that social stigma, and now these places are those where you find the last street-fashion trends or that previous book you cannot afford to buy brand-new. Do you remember that phone that was in the market three years ago and could resist a fall from the Moon? Why, instead of spending hours queueing for a Black Friday bargain, you spend that time cosy at home, browsing our website to see if there is something that could fit your needs without draining your wallet?

  We encourage you to use our website to post all those things you don’t need any more or browse in our product list to look for something that could fit your needs and can be collected near you. Your suggestions and comments are always welcomed and can be sent using our contact form.

The Experts' Opinion


“The stigma around secondhand electricals is probably not even related to an 'ewww' factor, it’s more that people think this stuff is already designed to break, so why bother with secondhand? Like anything else, there are more durable brands available from secondhand charities. Being an informed consumer counts for a lot.”

Janet Gunter, founder of The Restart Project

“Changing behaviour is vital and part of this process is encouraging people to become more conscious of the environmental and social impacts of their clothes, which in turn encourages them to value their clothes for longer. Looking to already existing resources to fulfil our needs is a powerful way to create a more sustainable society.”

Maria Chenoweth-Casey, CEO of Traid

“Fixing things gives you a thrill. There’s a sense of having beaten the system in some small way. I’m certain that this growing fixing movement is related to the changing attitudes to buying secondhand.”

Jane ni Dhulchaointigh, inventor and CEO of Sugru



“We buy things we don't need with money we don't have to impress people we don't like.”

Dave Ramsey, The Total Money Makeover: A Proven Plan for Financial Fitness

“It’s a slow process to get people to embrace secondhand. We can’t bully people to change their consumer habits overnight. Firstly we must give them beautiful, affordable, eco-friendly alternatives rather than trying to guilt trip them.”

Max McMurdo, founder of Reestore
